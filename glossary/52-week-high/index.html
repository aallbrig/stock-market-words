52-Week High — Stock Market Glossary

lastmod: 2026-03-20

52-Week High

The 52-week high is the highest price at which a stock has traded during the previous 52 weeks (one year). It is a widely used reference point for gauging a stock’s current price relative to its annual peak.

Why It Matters

The 52-week high acts as a significant psychological resistance level. Many investors and algorithms watch it closely:

  • When a stock breaks above its 52-week high, it often triggers momentum buying — traders interpret it as a sign of strength
  • When a stock is trading far below its 52-week high, it may be seen as either a bargain or a falling knife, depending on the reason

How to Use It

ScenarioInterpretation
Price near 52-week highStrong uptrend; potential breakout candidate
Price = 52-week high (new high)Momentum signal; often bullish
Price 10–20% below highModest pullback; still healthy trend
Price 30–50%+ below highSignificant decline; high risk and potential opportunity

52-Week High and the Falling Knife Concept

A stock trading significantly below its 52-week high has “fallen” — but that doesn’t automatically make it cheap or a buy. The market may be pricing in deteriorating fundamentals, sector headwinds, or broader economic concerns.

The 🔪 Falling Knife strategy on this site specifically looks for stocks well below their 52-week high that are also oversold by RSI and trading below their 200-day moving average. The thesis is that some of these stocks will recover — but catching a falling knife is inherently risky.

How It’s Used on This Site

The 52-week high appears in the Market Data card on every individual ticker page, alongside the 52-week low to show the full annual price range.

Related Terms

  • 52-Week Low — The other end of the annual range
  • Moving Average (200-Day) — Long-term trend context
  • RSI — Momentum indicator; high RSI near 52-week high
  • Beta — High-beta stocks have wider 52-week ranges
